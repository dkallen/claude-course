# **Introduction**

This framework simplifies organizational and project analysis by focusing on five core, interconnected concepts: **Goals**, **Problems**, **Solutions**, and their relational links. It employs a **hierarchical structure** for deep-dive analysis and serves as a **diagnostic tool** to ensure strategic alignment and clarify stakeholder thinking, drawing inspiration from the principles of the Theory of Constraints (TOC).

## **1\. Goals (The Desired State)**

**Definition:** Statements describing the desired outcomes, targets, or long-term vision for the business, project, or system. They define **what** we want to achieve.

* **Hierarchy:** Goals exist in a strict hierarchy. Lower-Level Goals are specific steps that, when achieved, **contribute directly** to the achievement of the broader, Higher-Level Goals. There is **no fixed number of levels**.

## **2\. Problems (The Current Undesired State)**

**Definition:** The current undesirable conditions, barriers, or gaps between the current state and the desired Goal state.

* **Hierarchy/Causality:** Problems exist in a causal hierarchy where **Root Cause Problems** trigger **Effect Problems**.  
* **Interaction Types (Causal Logic):** Problems can interact in two ways:
  * **Conjunctive Causality (AND):** When *both* problems must exist together to cause a larger problem. Resolving either one eliminates the effect. *Example: Poor inventory system AND unreliable supplier together cause stockouts.*
  * **Disjunctive Causality (OR):** When *any one* of multiple problems can independently cause a larger problem. In theory, all sources must be eliminated; in practice, problems have different weights or significance. By identifying which contributing problems have the highest impact, you can prioritize cost-effective solutions that address the most significant factors. *Example: Programmer frustration caused by poor documentation OR unclear requirements OR slow tooling. Documentation may be the primary driver (high weight), so resolving it first provides the most relief regardless of the other factors.*
* **Goal Obstacles:** A Problem is defined as an **obstacle** preventing the achievement of one or more specific Goals.

## ---

**3\. Solutions (The Actions to Achieve Goals)**

**Definition:** Specific, implementable actions, changes, or projects designed to **achieve Goals**.

* **Dual Purpose of Solutions:** Solutions serve two primary functions:  
  1. **Problem Resolution:** They are directed to address and **resolve underlying Problems** (obstacles), thereby removing the barriers to achieving Goals.  
  2. **Direct Goal Advancement:** They are created to **achieve a Goal directly**, even if no explicit Problem has been identified (e.g., proactive innovation).  
* **Hierarchy/Relationship to Goals:** Solutions are the actionable, **lower-level components** of the Goal hierarchy. The distinction between a lower-level Goal and a Solution is relative and based on **convention and judgment**.

## ---

**4\. Risks (The Forecasted Problems)**

**Definition:** **Forecasted** undesirable consequences that **could** arise as a direct result of implementing a specific Solution.

* **Nature:** Risks are potential, unintended future conditions (i.e., **forecasted Problems**).  
* **Purpose:** Identifying Risks is crucial to devise **mitigation plans** to minimize the probability or impact of the unintended consequences of the proposed Solution.

# ---

 **Framework Flow, Alignment, and Scope**

### **Solution Alignment Check**

When a Solution is proposed, it must pass a rigorous alignment test using the framework:

1. Is the Solution **resolving an identified Problem** that obstructs a Goal?  
2. Is the Solution **furthering a Goal directly**?  
3. If neither, the Solution is potentially **misaligned** with the scope of the current initiative, indicating:  
   * The framework may need to be used as a **thinking tool** to reveal an **unexpressed Goal or Problem** the stakeholder believes is important.  
   * The Solution may truly be **not useful** to the current effort and should be discarded or deferred.

### **The Critical Concept of Scope**

**Definition:** **Scope** defines the boundaries of the initiative, project, or analysis effort, determining which Goals, Problems, and Solutions are relevant and appropriate for consideration.

* **Exclusion:** Many important Goals and Problems may exist outside the boundaries of the current initiative and must be considered **out of scope**.  
* **Decision-Making:** The process of defining and adjusting the Scope (deciding what is in vs. out) is the crucial responsibility of the **stakeholders and decision-makers**. The framework helps ensure that all proposed work is tethered to the approved Scope.

### **Summary**

The framework operates as a continuous diagnostic and planning tool: **Diagramming** the connections between Goals, Problems, Solutions, and Risks is an essential exercise that helps **reveal confusion**, validate alignment, and ensures all efforts are strategically focused within the agreed-upon **Scope**.

# **Diagramming**

The most effective way to visualize the framework is through a **directed graph** structured hierarchically. In this visual model, the "Desired State" (Goals) sits at the top, while the detailed work (Solutions) anchors the bottom. The flow of logic—and the arrows in the diagram—should generally move **upwards**, indicating contribution, support, or resolution.

## **1\. Goal Hierarchy (Bottom-to-Top)**

Goals define what we want to achieve. In the diagram, the ultimate strategic vision is placed at the very top. Contributing (lower-level) goals are placed beneath them.

* **Direction:** Arrows point **upwards** from the lower goal to the higher goal, indicating that the lower goal *contributes* to the achievement of the top goal.  
* **Example:** If the primary goal is **"Be Healthy,"** it sits at the top. Contributing goals such as **"Exercise,"** **"Sleep Well,"** and **"Eat Well"** are arranged underneath. Arrows point from "Exercise" up to "Be Healthy."

## **2\. Problems as Obstacles**

Problems are defined as obstacles that prevent the achievement of specific goals.

* **Placement:** A problem is positioned directly **underneath** the specific goal it obstructs.  
* **Direction:** An arrow points **upwards** from the Problem to the Goal, indicating "interferes with" or "blocks."  
* **Example:** If you have a problem **"Injured Foot,"** it is placed below the goal **"Exercise."** An arrow points from "Injured Foot" up to "Exercise" to show it is directly blocking that specific contributing goal.

## **3\. Solutions as Resolutions**

Solutions are actionable steps designed to resolve underlying problems.

* **Placement:** Solutions are placed **underneath** the problems they address.  
* **Direction:** An arrow points **upwards** from the Solution to the Problem, indicating that the solution "resolves" or "removes" the obstacle.  
* **Example:** A proposed solution, **"Do exercises that don't involve the foot"** (e.g., swimming), is placed below **"Injured Foot."** The arrow points upward to show it addresses the injury limitation.

## **4\. Risks and Mitigations**

Risks are forecasted problems that arise as a direct result of a Solution.

* **Placement:** Because a risk opposes a solution (just as a problem opposes a goal), the Risk is positioned to point **at** the Solution.  
* **Direction:** An arrow points from the Risk to the Solution, indicating "interferes with" or "threatens."  
* **Mitigation:** You can then propose a **Mitigating Solution** placed below the Risk, with an arrow pointing up to the Risk to show it minimizes that threat.
